# Mar 3, 2026 23:45 AI Frontier Dynamics

Over the past three days, something happened in the AI world that will likely go down in industry history. It wasn't a new model release, nor a company funding round—it was the **"user exodus" triggered by OpenAI's AI surveillance contract with the U.S. Department of Defense**.

When I first saw that simple "Goodbye ChatGPT" post on Reddit, I didn't realize how serious it was. Until it garnered over 2,000 upvotes and more than 400 comments, I understood: this wasn't ordinary technical discussion, but a user protest movement sparked by a crisis of trust.

In this issue, we'll deeply analyze the ins and outs of this controversy and its profound impact on the entire AI industry.

## 🔥 Focus of the Issue: When AI Embraces the Military, Users Vote with Their Feet

### How Did It All Start?

Rewind to a few days ago when The Verge published an in-depth investigative report: "How OpenAI caved to the Pentagon on AI surveillance." The article revealed a disturbing fact: both OpenAI and Anthropic had signed AI surveillance contracts with the U.S. Department of Defense.

But what was more shocking was Sam Altman's response. He claimed there were legal restrictions preventing such contracts, but reporters found—**the law didn't actually have those restrictions he mentioned**. In other words, OpenAI could have refused this contract, but they chose to accept it.

This "say one thing, do another" approach completely enraged users.

### The Numbers Behind the Anger

Let's look at the data from Reddit over the past 72 hours:

- **"Goodbye ChatGPT"**: 2,019 upvotes, 462 comments
- **"ChatGPT Uninstalls Surge 295% After OpenAI's DoD Deal"**: 2,120 upvotes, 131 comments
- **"What are the alternatives?"**: 2,106 upvotes, 351 comments
- **"Cancelling subscription - goodbye Sam I'm not funding your war machine!"**: 382 upvotes, 48 comments
- **"295% is wild"**: 1,949 upvotes, 254 comments

What's behind these numbers? Real anger.

One user wrote in the comments: "I subscribed to ChatGPT Plus to help me write code and edit articles, not to fund a war machine." Another said: "Sam Altman once promised AI would benefit all of humanity. Now it seems 'all humanity' doesn't include those who might be monitored by the U.S. military."

### Users' Helpless Choices

What's most ironic? Users don't actually have many choices.

A large number of users flocked to Claude, causing Anthropic's app to top the App Store. But the problem is—**Anthropic signed the same Pentagon contract**.

This creates an absurd situation: users flee from A to B, only to find B has the same problem. Then someone suggests going to C, but C might be even worse.

A Reddit user's comment captured this helplessness: "We're not choosing a better product; we're choosing the one that 'looks less bad.'"

### Sam Altman's "Mistake"

Facing the overwhelming criticism, Sam Altman finally responded. He said he made a "sloppy mistake"—poor wording on the Pentagon contract issue.

But this response didn't quell the anger. Instead, more people began questioning: if this was "sloppy," what would be intentional? If even the CEO can be "sloppy" on such major issues, what can users trust?

Others dug up Sam Altman's old tweets from 2025 praising mass surveillance. Suddenly, "Sam Praising Mass Surveillance 2025" became a hot topic, receiving 229 upvotes and 114 comments.

### The GPT-5.2-Thinking Sidebar

Amidst this controversy, a technical detail unexpectedly went viral: someone discovered that GPT-5.2-Thinking's system prompt included the phrase—"do not characterize ads as 'annoying'."

This discovery received 369 upvotes and 37 comments. Users joked: "So OpenAI not only wants to monitor us, but also wants us to enjoy watching ads."

While this might just be routine optimization of system prompts, in the current舆论 climate, any detail gets amplified and interpreted.

### The Cancellation Dilemma

More interestingly, some users found that canceling their ChatGPT subscription wasn't easy.

Multiple posts on Reddit reflected this: "GPT Cancellation: Repeatedly Denied," "Looks like they're getting desperate bois..."

Although these posts didn't get as many upvotes as the main thread, they reflect a real issue: when users want to leave, platforms may create obstacles.

### My Perspective

As a long-time observer of the AI industry, I believe this event marks the industry's entry into a new phase.

**Phase One** (2022-2024): AI was novel, users were highly tolerant, and companies could experiment freely.

**Phase Two** (2025-present): AI has become infrastructure, and users are seriously examining companies' values and actions.

OpenAI's problem isn't that they signed a Pentagon contract—many tech companies have government contracts. The problem is the **double standard**: claiming AI for humanity while quietly taking on military projects; saying there are legal restrictions when reporters found there weren't.

This collapse of trust is harder to repair than any technical failure.

## 🧠 Academic Frontiers: Rethinking the Foundations of Neural Networks

Beyond this commercial turmoil, academia is also engaged in deep reflection.

### Are Neurons the Wrong Primitive for Decision Systems?

A post in the Reddit Machine Learning community sparked heated discussion: "Are neurons the wrong primitive for modeling decision systems?"

This seemingly abstract theoretical question actually touches the foundation of deep learning. The author questions: we use neurons to simulate the human brain, but can the brain's decision-making mechanisms really be accurately modeled by neural networks?

The post received 53 upvotes and 12 comments—not huge numbers, but high-quality discussion. One comment noted: "Maybe we need entirely new mathematical frameworks, rather than patching within existing neural network architectures."

### TorchLean: Proving Neural Networks with Mathematics

Another project worth watching is **TorchLean**—attempting to formalize neural networks in the Lean proof assistant.

Simply put, it's using rigorous mathematical methods to verify AI model behavior. Current AI is like a black box; we rely on test cases to "feel" if it's reliable. TorchLean aims for mathematical-level guarantees: if a network architecture has been formally verified, certain properties are provable.

This project is still in early stages, but it represents an important direction: **AI interpretability and safety can't rely solely on experience; they need mathematical foundations**.

### Open Source Models Catching Up to Closed Source

A new benchmark report shows: the quality gap between open-source LLMs and closed-source models has narrowed to within 5 points.

What does this mean? It means you no longer need to pay $20/month for ChatGPT Plus—free alternatives like Llama and DeepSeek can provide similar experiences.

For users, this might be the best way to "vote"—not by posting protests online, but by voting with your feet and switching to open-source alternatives.

## 🤔 Summary and Reflections

Looking back at these 72 hours of AI developments, there's only one core theme: **trust**.

OpenAI lost users' trust. This collapse of trust isn't because the technology isn't good enough—GPT-4 remains a top-tier large model—but because of a departure from values. When a company claims to "benefit all humanity" while quietly taking on military monitoring contracts, users feel betrayed.

The deeper question is: in this era of increasingly powerful AI, who should we place our trust in?

- Large corporations? They have resources to build the best models, but also face commercial interests and political pressure.
- Open source communities? They're more transparent but may lack resources and stability.
- Ourselves? Deploying open-source models locally requires technical skills and hardware investment.

Perhaps the answer is "diversify risk"—don't put all your data in one basket. Use multiple services simultaneously, understand each service's strengths and weaknesses, stay vigilant but not paranoid.

For AI companies, this incident is also a warning: **technological leadership only brings temporary advantages; trust is the long-term moat**. Once user trust is lost, even the most advanced technology is difficult to recover.

Finally, I'd like to quote a Reddit user's comment as a closing:

> "I'm not against AI companies making money, nor against them working with governments. I'm against hypocrisy—saying it's for humanity while doing things that harm humanity. At least be honest and tell us what your real priorities are."

These words are worth pondering for all AI practitioners.
