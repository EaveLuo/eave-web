### My Thoughts: Once Trust Is Lost, It's Hard to Rebuild

As a long-time observer of the AI industry, I believe this event marks the industry's entry into a new phase.

**Phase One** (2022-2024): AI was novel, users were highly tolerant, and companies could experiment freely.

**Phase Two** (2025-present): AI has become infrastructure, and users are seriously examining companies' values and actions.

OpenAI's problem isn't that they signed a Pentagon contract — many tech companies have government contracts. The problem is the **double standard**: Claiming AI for humanity while quietly taking on military projects; saying there are legal restrictions when reporters found there weren't.

This collapse of trust is harder to repair than any technical failure.

More importantly, this event exposes a structural problem in the entire AI industry: When a handful of companies monopolize the most advanced technology, users don't actually have many choices. Even if you're unhappy with a particular company, where can you go?

This is why open-source models are so important — they provide genuine alternatives, freeing users from vendor lock-in.

## 💡 Open Source Highlights: Deep Dive into Trending GitHub Projects

### SGLang: High-Performance LLM Serving Framework

**Project Link**: https://github.com/sgl-project/sglang

SGLang is a high-performance serving framework designed for large language models and multimodal models. The project currently has 24,020 stars on GitHub, gaining approximately 31 stars daily, with 4,630 forks.

**Core Features**:
- Efficient inference engine supporting multiple model architectures
- Multimodal support, not limited to text generation
- Flexible deployment options, supporting both cloud and local environments

**Why It's Worth Watching**:

As large model applications become widespread, how to efficiently deploy and serve these models has become a critical issue. SGLang provides a complete solution, making it easier for developers to put models into production environments.

Particularly worth mentioning is SGLang's excellent support for the Qwen series models. Against the backdrop of the Qwen3.5 release, this framework's importance is further elevated.

### Repomix: Codebase Packaging Magic Tool

**Project Link**: https://github.com/yamadashy/repomix

Repomix is a powerful tool that packs entire codebases into a single AI-friendly file. The project has 22,190 stars on GitHub, gaining approximately 37 stars daily, with 1,028 forks.

**Core Functions**:
- Compress entire codebases into a single file
- Preserve code structure and comments
- Optimize formatting for easier LLM processing

**Application Scenarios**:

Imagine you want AI to help you analyze a large project's code structure. The traditional approach is copying and pasting files one by one, which is tedious and prone to omissions. Repomix can accomplish this task with one click, generating a single file containing all code, convenient for feeding directly to ChatGPT, Claude, or other AI tools.

This project's popularity reflects an important current trend: Developers increasingly rely on AI to assist with code understanding and refactoring, and how to make AI process large codebases more efficiently has become a practical need.

### TensorRT-LLM: NVIDIA's Inference Acceleration Tool

**Project Link**: https://github.com/NVIDIA/TensorRT-LLM

TensorRT-LLM is a toolkit launched by NVIDIA for accelerating large language model inference. The project has 12,991 stars and 2,139 forks.

**Technical Advantages**:
- Utilizes NVIDIA GPU Tensor Cores for acceleration
- Supports mainstream model architectures (GPT, LLaMA, Falcon, etc.)
- Provides Python API for easy integration

**Practical Significance**:

For enterprises needing to deploy large models, inference cost is a key consideration. TensorRT-LLM can significantly improve inference speed and reduce hardware costs. Against the backdrop of efficient models like Qwen3.5 being released, combined with TensorRT-LLM optimization, enterprises can deploy high-quality AI services at lower costs.

### Rig: LLM Application Framework in Rust

**Project Link**: https://github.com/0xPlaygrounds/rig

Rig is a modular, scalable LLM application development framework written in Rust. The project has 6,231 stars, gaining approximately 10 stars daily, with 676 forks.

**Why Choose Rust?**

Rust is known for its memory safety and high performance, making it very suitable for building AI applications requiring high concurrency and low latency. Rig fills a gap in the Rust ecosystem for LLM application development.

**Core Design Philosophy**:
- Modular architecture with pluggable components
- Type safety, catching errors at compile time
- Async support, efficiently handling concurrent requests

For developers pursuing ultimate performance, Rig offers a new option worth exploring.

### Tiny-LLM: Learning LLM Inference from Scratch

Tiny-LLM is a tutorial project for systems engineers, teaching you how to build a mini vLLM + Qwen from scratch on Apple Silicon. The project has 3,845 stars, gaining approximately 12 stars daily, with 283 forks.

**Educational Value**:

For developers wanting to deeply understand LLM inference principles, this is an excellent learning resource. The project doesn't rely on complex frameworks but starts from basic concepts, step by step building a usable inference engine.

### Vibe Coding: A New Programming Paradigm

A post on the Reddit ChatGPT community titled "Day One of Vibe Coding" received 1,262 upvotes and 37 comments, introducing a new programming approach — Vibe Coding.

What is Vibe Coding? Simply put, it's letting AI take over most of the coding work while humans only need to provide high-level direction and feedback. This approach emphasizes "vibe" and "atmosphere" rather than traditional precise control.

This concept sparked heated discussion. Supporters believe it represents the future of programming, allowing more people to create software using natural language. Critics worry it will degrade code quality and cause programmers to lose deep understanding of systems.

My view is: Vibe Coding will indeed lower the barrier to entry, but for complex systems, deep engineering thinking remains essential. The best approach may be combining both — using Vibe Coding for rapid prototyping, and traditional methods for polishing core logic.

## 🤔 Reflections: The AI Industry at a Crossroads

Standing at this point in March 2026, I feel the AI industry is at an important crossroads.

### Deep Causes of the Trust Crisis

The OpenAI trust crisis is not an accidental event; it's an inevitable result of industry development. When AI moves from labs to the masses, from toys to infrastructure, users naturally demand higher standards: not just usability, but trustworthiness.

The question is, can current commercial AI models meet this trust requirement?

A fundamental contradiction of closed-source models is: You must trust that companies will responsibly use your data, honestly represent their actions, and put user interests first. But when a company's business model conflicts with these promises, what happens?

The OpenAI Pentagon contract controversy provides an answer.

### The Renaissance of Open Source

It's against this backdrop that open source AI is experiencing a renaissance.

Qwen3.5's success is not isolated. It represents a new possibility: High-quality, self-deployable, transparent AI models. Users no longer need to hand their data over to third parties or accept opaque product decisions.

Of course, the open source model also has its challenges: Less clear business models than closed source, potentially insufficient technical support, ecosystems that need time to build. But these challenges are gradually being overcome.

### The Shadow of Geopolitics

The AWS data center attack incident reminds us that AI infrastructure has become part of geopolitics.

When data centers become military targets, when AI companies get involved in international conflicts, the entire industry needs to rethink: How do we survive and develop in this environment?

Decentralization may be an answer. Don't put all your eggs in one basket — whether geographically or in terms of technology stack diversification.

### Advice for Users

Facing this complex environment, I offer the following advice to ordinary users:

**1. Don't Over-Rely on a Single Platform**

No matter how much you like a product, be prepared with backup plans. Learn to use multiple AI tools and understand their strengths and weaknesses.

**2. Pay Attention to Open Source Options**

Open source models like Qwen3.5 have reached very high standards. If you have some technical ability, try deploying models locally and take control of your data and experience.

**3. Maintain Critical Thinking**

Don't blindly believe any company's marketing claims. Pay attention to independent evaluations and community feedback to form your own judgments.

**4. Value Privacy Protection**

When using AI services, pay attention to protecting your privacy. Avoid revealing sensitive information to AI, and understand platforms' privacy policies.

### Looking Ahead

In the short term, OpenAI's trust crisis will continue to ferment. User attrition, declining reputation, increased regulatory pressure — these are foreseeable outcomes.

In the medium term, we may see changes in market dynamics. Anthropic may continue to benefit, Google may increase investment, and open source models may gain more attention.

In the long term, this event may become a turning point in AI industry history. It marks the end of an era — the era when users unconditionally trusted tech giants — and heralds the beginning of a new era: one that is more diverse, more transparent, and more focused on user rights.

As AI users and observers, we are fortunate to witness this historical moment. Let's maintain curiosity, maintain criticism, and maintain hope.
