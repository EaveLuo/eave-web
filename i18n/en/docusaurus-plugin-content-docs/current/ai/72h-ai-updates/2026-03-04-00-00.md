## I. OpenAI's DoD Partnership Sparks Major Controversy

### Event Recap

The most explosive news in the AI world over the past 72 hours has been the revelation of OpenAI's partnership with the US Department of Defense (DoD). This news hit like a depth charge, generating over 2,000 discussion threads on Reddit's r/ChatGPT subreddit, with related topics receiving more than 2,000 upvotes.

According to reports, ChatGPT's uninstall rate **surged by 295%** after the news broke. What does this number mean? Simply put, nearly 1 out of every 4 users chose to completely delete the app. This level of user attrition is unprecedented in ChatGPT's history.

### Sam Altman's "Mistake"

What's even more interesting is Sam Altman's own reaction. He admitted on social media that this was a "sloppy mistake." But here's the question: Was this really a "mistake"? Or was it a strategic choice OpenAI felt compelled to make under commercial pressure?

From an observer's perspective, this looks more like crisis PR management. When a company whose mission is to "benefit humanity" is discovered to be deeply collaborating with the military—regardless of the collaboration's content—it touches users' sensitive nerves. After all, people use ChatGPT to get help, learn, and create—not to indirectly support military operations.

### User Reactions: From Anger to Action

The reactions on Reddit were very authentic. One user posted "Goodbye ChatGPT," which received over 2,000 upvotes and 400+ comments. Another user's post "What are the alternatives?" garnered even more attention with 2,200+ upvotes, indicating that a large number of users are seriously considering migrating to other platforms.

Behind this sentiment lies users' deep concerns about AI companies' values. When people entrust their data and conversation records to an AI system, they want to trust that company. A partnership with the military undoubtedly shakes the foundation of that trust.

### My Take

Honestly, my feelings about this incident are complex. On one hand, I understand that OpenAI, as a commercial company, needs to make money, and government partnerships represent a huge revenue source. On the other hand, I believe AI companies need to find a balance between commercial interests and user trust.

This incident serves as a wake-up call for all AI companies: In this era of information transparency, any major decision will be examined under a microscope. Transparency beforehand is better than apologies afterward.

## II. Anthropic's Love-Hate Relationship with the Pentagon

### Dramatic Twist

If OpenAI "actively walked into" controversy, then Anthropic "passively became famous." Just as OpenAI's DoD collaboration was exposed, the US Treasury announced it would terminate its use of Anthropic's Claude service. Ironically, this news actually made Claude unexpectedly popular.

A post on Reddit's r/Anthropic titled "Another day, another tweet from the Pentagon" received 380+ upvotes and 145 comments. The comment section was filled with support for Claude and mockery of the government's decision.

### Why Did Claude Become the "Victim"?

According to analysis, this may be related to Anthropic's long-standing commitment to AI safety. Anthropic has consistently emphasized AI alignment and safety research, even refusing to provide services for potentially harmful applications in certain cases. This "moral purity" might be seen as a disadvantage in commercial competition, but in this incident, it unexpectedly earned sympathy and support from users.

One user wrote in the comments: "If the government doesn't like Claude, that means Claude must be doing something right." While this logic is simple, it reflects the thoughts of many technical professionals—in an increasingly utilitarian tech world, sticking to principles is itself a scarce quality.

### Unexpected Marketing Effect

In a sense, this "ban" might be the best free advertising Anthropic could have asked for. Many users who had never heard of Claude started trying out this "government-banned AI." One user joked: "I feel like a rebel when I use Claude now."

This reminds me that in the competition for tech products, sometimes "taking sides" matters more than "features." When users feel a product represents certain values, they're more willing to pay for it and spread the word.

## III. Qwen 3.5: The Small Model Revolution

### Explosive Performance

Amidst all the controversial news, Alibaba's release of the Qwen 3.5 series felt refreshingly different. This release covered models ranging from 0.8B to 35B parameters, with the most surprising aspect being the performance of the smaller models.

According to community tests, Qwen 3.5's 0.8B version (less than 1 billion parameters) can actually match some 70B parameter models on certain tasks. What does this mean? It means you can run an AI assistant close to GPT-4 level on an ordinary smartphone.

### Significance of the Technical Breakthrough

This trend toward "small models with big performance" has profound implications for the entire AI industry:

1. **Lower Barriers to Entry**: No longer need expensive GPU servers; ordinary developers and even individual users can deploy high-quality AI locally
2. **Privacy Protection**: Data doesn't need to be uploaded to the cloud, fundamentally solving privacy leak risks
3. **Reduced Costs**: Inference costs drop significantly, facilitating the普及 of AI applications

### Real-World Testing

A developer shared their testing experience on Reddit. They ran Qwen 3.5's 4B version on a 2019 laptop, and the response speed was actually faster than some cloud-based large models. While there's still a gap in complex reasoning tasks, it's completely sufficient for daily conversations and text generation.

Even more impressive, someone successfully ran Qwen 3.5's 0.8B version in a browser—that's right, a regular Chrome browser, no backend server needed. This is simply great news for web developers.

### The Power of Open Source

Another highlight of Qwen 3.5 is that it's fully open source, including training code and detailed technical reports. This stands in sharp contrast to some companies' approach of "open weights but closed technical details."

In my view, this genuine spirit of open source is what drives AI progress. When knowledge can flow freely, innovation accelerates. The Qwen team's approach deserves respect.

## IV. Open-Source LLMs Approaching Proprietary Levels

### Surprising Benchmark Results

A research post on Reddit's r/MachineLearning attracted widespread attention: researchers tested 94 LLM endpoints and found that the quality gap between open-source and proprietary models has narrowed to within 5 percentage points.

This is a milestone achievement. Looking back a year ago, open-source models were still struggling to catch up to GPT-3.5's level, and now they're approaching GPT-4's performance.

### Reasons Behind the Progress

This rapid catch-up has several key factors:

1. **Better Training Methods**: RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) and similar techniques are no longer exclusive to proprietary models
2. **Synthetic Data**: High-quality synthetic training data greatly reduces reliance on expensive human labeling
3. **Community Collaboration**: Global developers jointly improve models, iterating far faster than any single company
4. **Democratized Computing Resources**: Cloud computing and distributed training allow more people to participate in large model R&D

### Implications for the Industry

As the gap between open-source and proprietary models narrows, competition in the AI field will become more intense. For ordinary users, this is good news—more choices, lower prices, better service.

But for companies that have built moats around their model advantages, the pressure will increase. When technical barriers gradually disappear, competition shifts to other dimensions: user experience, ecosystem, brand trust, etc.

### My Prediction

I suspect that within the next 12 months, we'll see the first open-source model that surpasses GPT-4 in comprehensive performance. Moreover, this model will likely come from a research team in China or Europe—because US export controls have actually stimulated innovation in other regions.

## V. GitHub Trending Projects Quick Look

### sglang: Efficient LLM Serving Framework

sglang is a new framework for large language model serving, focusing on high efficiency and low latency. Its design philosophy is quite interesting—by optimizing memory usage and batch processing strategies, it can support more concurrent requests on the same hardware.

For developers looking to build their own AI services, this project is worth watching. It might be more suitable than vLLM for certain specific scenarios.

### repomix: Package Your Entire Codebase for AI

repomix solves a very practical problem: when you want AI to help analyze your entire codebase, how do you organize all the files into a format that's easy for AI to understand?

This tool automatically traverses your project, organizes code files in a reasonable structure, and generates a large text file. You can directly throw this file at ChatGPT or Claude and have it help with code review, refactoring suggestions, or documentation generation.

### LLMs-from-scratch: Implementing Large Models from Scratch

This is an educational project that walks you through implementing a large language model from scratch. Not using pre-built packages, but truly understanding how each component works.

For those wanting to deeply understand Transformer architecture, this is one of the best learning materials available. The code is clear, well-commented, and comes with accompanying video tutorials.

## VI. TorchLean: Formal Verification of Neural Networks with Lean

### What is Formal Verification?

Simply put, it's using mathematical methods to rigorously prove program correctness. Traditional software testing can only prove that "the program works correctly under certain inputs," while formal verification can prove that "the program is correct under all possible inputs."

### Why Does This Matter for AI?

Neural networks have long been criticized as "black boxes"—we know they work, but we don't know why. This is a big problem in safety-critical applications. If you're going to use AI to control autonomous vehicles or medical devices, you definitely want to know it will make correct decisions in all situations.

The TorchLean project attempts to formalize certain properties of neural networks using Lean (a powerful theorem-proving language). Although still in early stages, this represents an important research direction.

### Technical Challenges

Formal verification of neural networks faces enormous challenges. The nonlinear characteristics of neural networks make traditional verification techniques difficult to apply. But as research progresses, we may see more breakthroughs.

Imagine future AI systems that not only pass tests but also come with mathematical proofs guaranteeing they won't make mistakes under specific conditions. This would be a major advancement in AI safety.

## VII. Vibe Coding: A New Programming Paradigm

### What is Vibe Coding?

This term has been trending on Twitter and Reddit lately. It refers to a relaxed, intuitive programming style—not pursuing perfect architecture, not obsessing over best practices, but going with the flow and iterating quickly.

Some translate it as "atmosphere programming" or "casual programming." The core idea is: in the era of AI-assisted programming, the human role is shifting from "precise instruction writer" to "creative guide."

### Comparison with Traditional Programming

Traditional programming emphasizes rigor: design first, then implement, write documentation, do testing, follow standards. Vibe Coding is more like improvisation: start with a rough idea, let AI help refine the details, adjust when problems arise.

Neither approach is absolutely better or worse. For exploratory projects or prototype development, Vibe Coding might be more efficient; for production-grade systems, traditional methods remain necessary.

### My Experience

Recently, I've also tried this programming approach. I have to say, with AI's help, development speed is indeed much faster. Features that used to take hours to write can now be done in half an hour.

But there's a cost—code quality varies, and sometimes refactoring is needed later. I think the ideal pattern is: use Vibe Coding to quickly validate ideas first, then use traditional methods to polish them into reliable products.
