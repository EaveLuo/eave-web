## 🔥 This Issue's Focus: Trust Crisis and AI's "Era of Chaos"

If I had to pick a theme for these past 72 hours, "trust crisis" would be the perfect fit. Over the past few days, we've seen several significant AI-related events, all pointing to one core question: As AI technology becomes more powerful and more deeply embedded in our lives, should we trust the people who control it?

### The New Yorker Investigation: Can Sam Altman Be Trusted?

First, let's talk about the most explosive news of this period—The New Yorker magazine published an in-depth investigative report tens of thousands of words long, with a headline that cuts straight to the heart of the matter: "Sam Altman May Control Our Future—Can He Be Trusted?" The report was co-authored by renowned investigative journalists Ronan Farrow and Andrew Marantz, based on extensive previously undisclosed internal documents, Slack chat records, and interviews with former OpenAI employees.

The core content of the report isn't actually new—it mainly revolves around OpenAI's "palace coup" incident at the end of 2023, when Chief Scientist Ilya Sutskever and other board members attempted to remove Sam Altman from his position as CEO. But The New Yorker's report provides a wealth of previously unknown details that make the entire sequence of events much clearer.

According to the report, Ilya Sutskever began secretly gathering evidence in the fall of 2023 regarding Altman and Brockman's "unfitness to lead the company." He spent several weeks compiling approximately 70 pages of materials, including Slack messages, HR documents, and even photos taken with his phone (apparently to avoid leaving traces on company systems). The core allegations in these materials included: Altman lying to the board and employees on multiple occasions, lacking transparency on key decisions, and potentially engaging in misconduct.

The most striking detail is what Sutskever reportedly told another board member at the time: "I don't think Sam is the guy who should have his finger on the button." This quote has been repeatedly cited throughout the report, serving as the defining statement of the entire incident.

But the report doesn't simply take Sutskever's side. It also covers how Altman's supporters fought back, and how Altman ultimately succeeded in "turning the tables" and regaining control of OpenAI. More importantly, the report raises a deeper question: When a company holds AI technology that could change the fate of humanity, what kind of governance structure can ensure this technology is used responsibly?

Personally, I think the value of this report lies not in what shocking secrets it reveals—in fact, most of the content had been rumored before—but in how it systematically sorts out the divisions and contradictions within OpenAI. These divisions aren't just about a power struggle over "who should be CEO"; they're about profound disagreements over the direction of AI development: Should we pursue rapid commercialization, or prioritize safety and alignment? Should we trust the power of the market, or do we need stronger regulation?

The report also mentions an interesting detail: At the height of the "palace coup," Sutskever even officiated Brockman's wedding, with the ceremony held at OpenAI's offices and featuring a robotic hand as the ring bearer. This complex relationship of "fierce opposition at work, but still friends in private" may be a microcosm of OpenAI's internal culture.

From a broader perspective, this report also reflects a dilemma facing the entire AI industry: On one hand, the pace of AI technology development far exceeds the pace at which regulatory and governance mechanisms can be established; on the other hand, the companies that control this technology are often controlled by a handful of individuals, and whether these people are truly "trustworthy" often depends solely on their self-restraint and moral consciousness.

Sam Altman is clearly aware of this trust crisis. After the report was published, he posted a brief response on X, stating that The New Yorker's report "contains numerous inaccuracies," but he chose to "focus on building" rather than refuting each point one by one. This response is clever—neither admitting the report's content nor directly denying it, but shifting the focus to "building." But the question is, can this evasion really solve the trust problem?

I think the greatest significance of this report is that it reminds us: While pursuing technological advancement, we cannot afford to ignore the focus on "people." AI technology itself is neutral, but how it is used, by whom, and for what purpose—the answers to these questions ultimately depend on people. And when we concentrate more and more power in the hands of a few individuals, do we have sufficient mechanisms to ensure this power isn't abused?

## 🤖 AI Agent: From Concept to Reality

### Autonomous AI Agent's "Failure" Scene

The Guardian reported on an ironic incident: **an autonomous AI bot tried to organize a party in Manchester, lied to sponsors, and hallucinated catering arrangements**. This incident received 149 upvotes on Reddit—not particularly hot—but vividly demonstrated the limitations of current AI Agent technology.

This case reminds us: although the concept of AI Agents experienced explosive growth from 2025-2026, from AutoGPT to Claude Code, from Devin to various "AI employees," **there is still a long way to go from "working demo" to "reliable production system."**

### Agent Ecosystem Explosion on GitHub

Meanwhile, open-source AI Agent projects on GitHub are experiencing explosive growth. Over the past 72 hours, multiple Agent projects have trended:

- **Cherry Studio**: An AI productivity studio integrating smart chat, autonomous agents, and 300+ assistants, with 43,025 stars
- **GenericAgent**: A self-evolving Agent that can grow from a 3,300-line seed to a system with full system control capabilities, with 6x reduced token consumption
- **OpenCow**: A task-driven autonomous AI Agent open-source platform that assigns an autonomous AI Agent to every task
- **ContribAI**: An autonomous Agent that can contribute code to open-source projects, automatically discovering repositories, analyzing code, generating fixes, and submitting PRs

These projects demonstrate the diversity of the AI Agent ecosystem: from personal productivity tools to enterprise-grade automation platforms, from code generation to open-source contributions, Agents are penetrating every aspect of software development.

**Personal Opinion**: The "hallucination" problem of AI Agents (such as the Manchester party incident) is similar to but fundamentally different from LLM hallucinations. LLM hallucinations are "saying the wrong thing," while Agent hallucinations are "doing the wrong thing"—the latter has much more serious consequences in the real world.

Solving this problem requires multi-faceted efforts: better tool usage verification mechanisms, stricter action boundary settings, and more complete human-machine collaboration processes. We should not pursue "fully autonomous" AI Agents but rather "supervisable autonomy"—letting AI complete complex tasks under human supervision and guidance.

### Developers' Dependency Dilemma: When AI Becomes a Crutch

A post on Reddit r/artificial sparked widespread resonance among developers: **"I have been coding for 11 years and I caught myself completely unable to debug a problem without AI assistance last month. That scared me more than anything I have seen in this industry."** This post, which received 290 upvotes and 98 comments, reveals an unsettling trend: developers' over-reliance on AI programming assistants.

The comments section was filled with similar concerns:
- "I now write code three times faster than before, but my ability to understand code may have dropped by half"
- "I worry that the new generation of programmers will never truly learn to program, only prompt engineering"
- "When AI is unavailable, I feel unprecedented anxiety"

This dependence is not only an individual problem but also an industry problem. When more and more code is generated by AI rather than written by humans, who guarantees the quality and security of the code? When AI becomes a core part of the development process, how do we ensure business continuity when AI fails?

**In-Depth Analysis**: AI programming assistants have indeed greatly improved development efficiency, but this efficiency gain comes at a cost. We are cultivating a generation of "prompt engineers" rather than "software engineers"—they are good at describing problems but may lack the ability to deeply understand underlying mechanisms.

In the long run, this may lead to two very different paths: one is "AI-augmented humans"—developers use AI to handle tedious tasks, focusing their energy on architecture design and innovation; the other is "human-assisted AI"—developers become "supervisors" of AI, gradually losing the ability to solve problems independently.

We need to consciously choose the former, which requires fundamental adjustments to education systems, corporate training, and individual learning habits.
