## 🔥 This Issue's Focus: The AI Agent Warring States Era Has Officially Begun

If I had to pick a theme for these 72 hours, "The Warring States Era of AI Agents" would be perfect. Every major player is accelerating their positioning, trying to grab a foothold in this explosive new赛道 before it takes off.

### Anthropic's Claude Can Now Control Your Computer

First, let's talk about Anthropic's big move. They announced on Monday (March 24) that Claude can now actually control users' computers to complete tasks. This feature sounds simple, but its implications are profound.

Picture this scenario: You oversleep one morning and are about to miss an important meeting. You quickly grab your phone and message Claude, asking it to export your PowerPoint as a PDF and add it to the meeting invite. By the time you finish getting ready, everything's already done.

That's exactly what Anthropic demonstrated in their release video. Claude can open applications on your computer, browse the web, fill out spreadsheets—essentially acting as a digital personal assistant.

This feature is actually a response to the viral OpenClaw that's been making waves recently. OpenClaw is an open-source AI Agent project that can receive instructions through WhatsApp or Telegram and execute tasks on users' devices. Its popularity has made the entire industry realize the massive potential of AI Agents.

Anthropic's move is, in a way, saying: "We can do this too, and we can do it better." After all, Claude is their own model, so the integration will definitely be tighter.

But here's an interesting question: When AI Agents can actually control our devices, how do we ensure security? Anthropic says Claude runs locally and doesn't send data to the cloud. But what does "local" really mean? What files can it access? Could it make mistakes? These are all concerns users will have.

My personal take is that AI Agents are definitely heading in the right direction. Humans shouldn't waste time on repetitive computer tasks—let AI handle those mundane things while we focus on more creative work. But before widespread adoption, we really need to put more effort into security and building user trust.

### OpenAI's Counterattack: ChatGPT Shopping Feature Goes Live

OpenAI certainly isn't sitting idle. Almost simultaneously, they launched ChatGPT's shopping feature, allowing users to browse and purchase products directly within ChatGPT.

The design philosophy behind this feature is quite interesting. OpenAI says traditional online shopping works like this: you already know what you want to buy, then you search, compare prices, and place an order. But often, we don't actually know exactly what we want—like "I want to buy a jacket suitable for spring outdoor activities." In these cases, we end up jumping between different websites, reading reviews, comparing prices—it's a hassle.

ChatGPT's shopping feature aims to solve this problem. You can describe your needs in natural language, and ChatGPT will help you find suitable products, displaying them in a visual format. You can upload an image as a reference to find similar styles, or compare different products side by side with their specs, prices, and reviews.

OpenAI built this feature on their "Agentic Commerce Protocol (ACP)." This protocol was developed to connect merchants with AI assistants, allowing product information to be displayed in ChatGPT more completely and in real-time.

What I find smartest about this feature is how it changes the shopping decision path. Traditional e-commerce is "search-filter-buy," while ChatGPT does "need understanding-recommendation-buy." For merchants, this means reaching users with clearer purchase intent; for consumers, it saves tons of time filtering through information.

However, this feature has also raised some concerns. When AI starts intervening in shopping decisions, will its recommendation algorithms be biased? Will it prioritize certain merchants' products? OpenAI needs to provide clear answers to these questions.

### OpenAI Foundation: $1 Billion Investment Plan

Beyond product updates, OpenAI also announced the latest developments from their foundation. They plan to invest at least $1 billion over the next year in areas including life sciences, disease treatment, employment and economic impact, AI resilience, and community programs.

Behind this plan is OpenAI's restructuring announced last year. They transformed from a non-profit organization into a "public benefit corporation," aiming to secure more funding while maintaining their mission.

OpenAI says their mission is to ensure artificial general intelligence (AGI) benefits all of humanity. This mission has two dimensions: first, using AI to solve humanity's most difficult problems; second, addressing the new challenges that AI brings.

One billion dollars sounds like a lot, but considering OpenAI's valuation is approaching one trillion dollars, this amount is actually a small fraction of their market cap. However, the move itself sends a signal: OpenAI wants to do more at the application level of AI, not just sell models.

My personal expectation for this foundation is that it can really drive some substantial progress, especially in healthcare and scientific discovery. AI has already shown tremendous potential in areas like protein folding prediction and drug development—if more resources are invested, we might actually see breakthroughs in these fields.

## 🚀 AI Product Updates

### Former Apple Designer Joins Hark to Build New AI Hardware

Speaking of AI hardware, there's some interesting news. Former Apple designer Abidur Chowdhury has joined Hark, an AI lab founded by serial entrepreneur Brett Adcock.

Chowdhury was responsible for industrial design of products like the iPhone Air while at Apple—definitely a top-tier designer in the industry. His decision to leave Apple for Hark shows just how compelling Hark's vision must be.

What does Hark want to build? According to their statement, they want to design an "end-to-end personal intelligence product"—including multimodal models, hardware, and interfaces, all designed in tandem to work seamlessly together. This system will have persistent memory and be able to listen, see, and interact with the world in real-time.

Brett Adcock wrote in an internal memo from January: "My view is simple: today's AI models aren't nearly intelligent enough, they feel quite dumb, and the devices we use to access them are fundamentally pre-AI. We're moving toward a world that looks more like sci-fi characters Jarvis or Her, with systems that anticipate, adapt, and genuinely care about the people using them."

This vision sounds beautiful, but achieving it will be extremely difficult. Apple spent over a decade getting the iPhone to where it is today. For Hark, a startup trying to design hardware for the AI era from scratch, the challenges are immense.

However, Chowdhury's addition does add significant credibility to the project. In an interview with TechCrunch, he said: "What was very clear for me at the time is that the world is clearly changing, but we're using the same devices... everything's been designed around these existing platforms. Very few people are really going after what the future is. There's so much that we could be doing if intelligence was at the base layer of every device."

Hark plans to release their first AI models this summer. I'm really looking forward to seeing what they come up with.

### New Faces on GitHub: SGLang and Repomix

Several interesting projects appeared on GitHub Trending this week.

**SGLang** is a high-performance serving framework for large language models and multimodal models. Its key features are speed and efficiency, making it suitable for production deployment. The project has already gained nearly 25,000 stars, showing strong community recognition.

**Repomix** is a fascinating tool that can package an entire codebase into a single AI-friendly file. Imagine you want an AI to help analyze a project, but the project has hundreds of files—copying and pasting everything is impractical. Repomix solves this problem by traversing the entire codebase and merging all files into a structured text file that's easy to feed to AI.

With over 22,000 stars, this project clearly addresses a real need many developers have. I tried it myself, and it's quite convenient. Especially when you want to quickly understand a new project—just package it with Repomix, throw it to an AI for analysis, and it's much more efficient than going through files one by one.

## 📰 Industry News

### Microsoft Threatens to Sue OpenAI: Partnership Shows Cracks

When it comes to the most explosive news this week, I'd say it's definitely Microsoft threatening to sue OpenAI.

The story began with a $50 billion partnership agreement between OpenAI and Amazon AWS. OpenAI planned to offer their new product "Frontier" on AWS, and Microsoft believes this violates their exclusivity agreement with OpenAI.

According to the Microsoft-OpenAI agreement, all API calls accessing OpenAI models should go through Microsoft's Azure cloud platform. But OpenAI seems to be trying to circumvent this clause by running Frontier directly on AWS.

A person familiar with Microsoft's position told the Financial Times: "We know our contract. If they breach it, we will sue them. If Amazon and OpenAI want to take a bet on the creativity of their contractual lawyers, I would back us, not them."

That's pretty harsh language. But the story behind it is even more complex.

The Microsoft-OpenAI relationship was a "mutual benefit" arrangement from the start. OpenAI needed Microsoft's money and computing power; Microsoft needed OpenAI's technology to compete with Google. But with ChatGPT's success and OpenAI's soaring valuation, they've started seeking greater independence.

Last year, when OpenAI restructured as a public benefit corporation, the two sides clashed. The eventual compromise was that Microsoft gave up its position as OpenAI's exclusive cloud provider but retained the clause requiring all API calls to go through Azure.

Now it seems OpenAI wants to further break free from Microsoft's control, and Microsoft clearly isn't willing to let go. After all, OpenAI has become one of Microsoft's biggest competitors—losing control completely would be a huge threat to Microsoft.

This development is worth watching closely. If the two sides do end up in court, it could have significant impacts on the entire AI industry's landscape.

### AI Sparks Heated Debate in Physics Community: Harvard Professor Claims AI Can Replace PhD Students

There's another fascinating news story this week from the physics community.

At the American Physical Society Global Physics Summit in Denver, AI became the hottest topic. Harvard professor Matthew Schwartz said in a presentation that Anthropic's Claude chatbot can solve advanced physics problems at the level of early-stage PhD students.

Even more surprising, Schwartz said he completed a research paper on quantum field theory in collaboration with Claude in about two weeks—a task that would have taken roughly two years with a student collaborator.

"AI puts theoretical physics on the chopping block," Schwartz said. He no longer mentors students who don't want to collaborate with AI tools and believes all AI-supporting researchers should learn to use these tools.

At the summit, I noticed many attendees using AI chatbots to help them understand presentation content. "What are transmon qubits?" "Explain spintronics to me." "What is a two-level system?" The AIs would immediately provide answers, even using emojis as bullet points.

This phenomenon sparked a profound discussion: Will AI change the nature of physics research? If AI can complete in weeks what used to take years, what happens to the role of human physicists?

My view is that AI will indeed change how scientific research is conducted, but it won't replace human scientists. Scientific research isn't just about solving problems—it also involves asking questions, designing experiments, and interpreting results. These tasks requiring creativity and intuition can't be replaced by AI in the short term. But AI can be a powerful tool, helping scientists validate ideas and process data more quickly.

### ICML 2026 Review Discussion Sparks Heated Debate

Reviewing for ICML 2026, one of the top conferences in machine learning, is underway, and discussions about the review process are heating up on Reddit.

One post that resonated widely asked: "Has industry effectively killed off academic machine learning research in 2026?"

This question reflects the growing gap between academia and industry. Industry has massive amounts of data and computing resources, enabling them to train enormous models; academia is limited by resources and struggles to compete with industry on model scale.

Many researchers in the discussion expressed that the current academic environment is increasingly difficult to survive in. Funding competition is fierce, publication pressure is high, and truly influential work often comes from big companies.

This is a question worth pondering. If academia can't keep pace with industry, who will be responsible for basic research? Who will explore directions that don't show commercial value in the short term?

## 💡 Open Source Highlights

### ZeroClaw Releases Multiple Versions in Rapid Succession

ZeroClaw (an open-source AI Agent platform) released multiple versions this week, from v0.5.5 to v0.6.1, with very frequent updates.

Key updates include:
- New emoji reaction tool supporting cross-channel reactions
- New session list, history, and send tools for inter-agent messaging
- Matrix protocol support for room-level access control
- Multimodal features: routing image messages to dedicated vision model providers
- Heartbeat feature: supporting injection of session history
- Memory architecture upgrade, removing mem0 backend

ZeroClaw's rapid development reflects the vibrancy of the open-source AI Agent ecosystem. Compared to closed-source solutions, open-source projects allow users to fully control their own data and models—this is very attractive for users who care about privacy.

### Cherry Studio: AI Productivity Studio

**Cherry Studio** is an AI productivity studio integrating smart chat, autonomous agents, and over 300 assistants. It supports unified access to frontier large language models and has a user-friendly interface design.

This project has gained significant attention in a short time, showing strong market demand for this kind of "all-in-one" AI workbench.

### ResearcherSkill: Turning AI Agents into Scientists

**ResearcherSkill** is a fascinating project that gives your AI coding agents (like Claude Code or Codex) the ability to conduct autonomous experiments.

In simple terms, you give the agent a research goal, and it automatically designs experiments, runs code, analyzes results, and adjusts strategies based on feedback. For research work requiring lots of trial and error, this could significantly improve efficiency.

### AI Misidentification Leads to Wrongful Imprisonment of Tennessee Grandmother

Speaking of AI's negative impacts, there's a disturbing news story this week.

A Tennessee grandmother was wrongfully imprisoned for six months due to an AI facial recognition system error. This is the latest case of AI-driven misidentification, yet police continue to use this technology.

This case reminds us that AI technology can make serious errors in real-world applications, especially when those errors involve the justice system—the consequences can be disastrous.

Facial recognition technology can indeed improve law enforcement efficiency under ideal conditions, but if accuracy isn't high enough, innocent people will be wrongfully accused. Before deploying such technologies, we need stricter testing and regulation.

## 🤔 This Issue's Reflections

Writing this, I'd like to share some thoughts that impressed me most this week.

First, about the development direction of AI Agents. Both Anthropic and OpenAI are accelerating their positioning, and OpenClaw's viral success has ignited enthusiasm across the entire industry. But I'm concerned: are we pursuing "agents that can do more things" while neglecting "agents that should do the right things"?

When AI Agents can control our devices and access our files, security and privacy protection become crucial. Technology often develops faster than regulatory and ethical frameworks, which can bring risks.

Second, about the relationship between Microsoft and OpenAI. This "frenemy" story tells us that in the business world, there are no permanent friends, only permanent interests. When OpenAI was weak, Microsoft was a lifeline; when OpenAI became strong, Microsoft became a stumbling block.

This evolution also reflects changes in the AI industry's power dynamics. A few years ago, tech giants like Microsoft and Google were absolute dominators; now, AI-native companies like OpenAI are starting to challenge their positions.

Finally, about AI's impact on academia. While the Harvard professor's remarks sounded radical, they did point to a trend: AI is becoming a standard tool for scientific research. Future scientists may really need to learn to collaborate with AI, just as today's scientists need to learn to use computers.

But this doesn't mean human scientists will be replaced. The core of scientific research is asking questions and creating knowledge—these require human intuition, creativity, and value judgment. AI can accelerate the process but cannot replace the purpose.
