## 🔥 Spotlight: OpenAI's "Strategic Contraction" and the AI Industry Reshuffle

If I had to give a theme to these past 72 hours, "strategic contraction" would be most appropriate. OpenAI, the leader in the AI industry, announced the shutdown of Sora video generation tool and abandoned its adult chatbot plan within a single week. This posture of "doing less" is quite rare in recent years. What exactly happened behind the scenes? And what does it mean?

### OpenAI Shuts Down Sora: The Harsh Reality of the Video Generation Race

First, let's talk about Sora. This AI video generation tool that once carried high hopes was shut down by OpenAI less than a year after launch. The official statement was brief: "We're saying goodbye to Sora. To everyone who created with Sora, shared it, and built community around it: thank you." But the reasons behind this decision are thought-provoking.

The problems with Sora actually had early warning signs. While it could generate videos that looked realistic, the question was—are these videos actually useful? For professional creators, the quality of Sora-generated videos didn't meet commercial standards; for ordinary users, the novelty wore off after a few tries. More importantly, the video generation space is already crowded with competitors: Runway, Pika, Kling—each is fighting for a piece of this pie.

But perhaps the most fatal blow came from Disney's withdrawal. Originally, Disney planned to invest $1 billion in OpenAI, allowing Sora to generate "fan-inspired" videos using over 200 characters from Disney, Marvel, Pixar, and Star Wars. But now, with Sora's shutdown, this investment has fallen through. Disney's statement was official: "As the AI field advances rapidly, we respect OpenAI's decision to exit the video generation business." But anyone with eyes can see that if Sora truly had value, Disney wouldn't easily abandon it.

I think Sora's shutdown actually reflects a deeper problem: the gap between AI products' "toy attributes" and "tool attributes." Many AI products look cool and can generate impressive content, but the question is—what practical problems do they solve? For most people, generating an AI video versus an AI image doesn't fundamentally differ in use cases. Moreover, video generation costs more and quality is harder to control, which greatly diminishes its practicality.

OpenAI says they want to focus on "core productivity tools," such as coding assistants. This strategic adjustment actually makes sense. After all, AI that can help programmers write code is more valuable to OpenAI's business model than AI that can generate videos. But this also means OpenAI is transforming from a company that "explores various AI possibilities" to one that "focuses on making money." This transition itself is understandable, but it may disappoint those who expected OpenAI to bring more "black tech" innovations.

### Adult Chatbot Plan Aborted: The Interplay of Ethics and Business

If Sora's shutdown was a business decision, the cancellation of the adult chatbot plan is more like the result of a battle between ethics and business.

This feature, reportedly called "Citron mode," was originally planned for release last December but was delayed repeatedly and finally "indefinitely shelved" this month. OpenAI's reason was: they want to conduct long-term research on the effects of erotic chat on users, and there currently isn't enough "empirical evidence" on the subject.

But Financial Times reporting revealed more insider details. Apparently, OpenAI encountered difficulties in training models—they needed to enable adult conversations while avoiding illegal content (such as bestiality, incest, etc.), and this balance is hard to strike. More critically, investors and employees expressed concerns about this feature, with some senior employees even leaving the company over the issue.

These concerns are not unfounded. Competitor xAI's Grok model previously fell into controversy for generating deepfake nudes of real people and children. OpenAI clearly doesn't want to repeat this mistake. Moreover, "AI shouldn't replace your human relationships"—this is the voice from within OpenAI, and the consensus of many people.

I think this decision is actually the right one. Although adult content has a huge market demand, OpenAI as an influential AI company does need to consider social responsibility. If AI chatbots make people increasingly dependent on virtual "intimate relationships" rather than real human interaction, this may not be a good thing for society. Moreover, once any scandal occurs, the damage to OpenAI's brand could be irreparable.

But this also raises an interesting question: where exactly is the boundary of AI "content moderation"? OpenAI says Citron mode is "indefinitely shelved," not "permanently canceled," which indicates they haven't completely ruled out the possibility of future release. Perhaps when they find better technical solutions, or when society's acceptance of this issue increases, this feature will return. But until then, OpenAI chose caution.

### Anthropic's "Double-Edged News": Model Leak and Legal Victory

Meanwhile, OpenAI's main competitor Anthropic also dominated headlines, and in a "double-edged" manner.

First, the bad news: one of Anthropic's new models leaked accidentally. This model named "Mythos" was described as "the most capable model Anthropic has ever built," representing a "step change" in AI capabilities. The leak was quite embarrassing—relevant description documents were stored in a publicly accessible data cache and discovered by security researchers.

Although Anthropic quickly removed these documents, this leak still exposed an awkward fact: even companies focused on AI safety can make mistakes in basic information security. Nearly 3,000 unpublished blog posts and assets were publicly accessible—this is inexcusable by any measure. Anthropic attributed this incident to "human error in content management system configuration," but this also reminds everyone: in the AI era, data security is more important than ever.
